We are drowning in data and starving for understanding.

Bad decisions aren't made from bad data. They're made from good data,
poorly interpreted. The bottleneck has never been information. It has
always been the framework for making sense of it.

That conviction has shaped every chapter.

---

## Systems Engineering

Joshua M. Peck began his career as a systems engineer in academic
computing — building the infrastructure that connected universities
across Kansas to shared networks, authentication systems, and digital
resources. The work required something specific: systems that behaved
correctly regardless of what the environment was doing. Unpredictable
users, unpredictable load, unpredictable failure modes. The job was
to build something that worked anyway.

That discipline carried forward. As CTO of a web startup in the early
2000s, Peck built a consolidated yield management platform applying
early machine learning to advertising data — tracking revenue and
spend to the keyword level, detecting anomalies, expanding what
worked. The company grew from zero to $30 million in revenue in
roughly eighteen months. The machine learning worked because the
problem was framed correctly: not prediction, but detection. Not
forecasting outcomes, but identifying structure in data that was
always changing.

The lesson from this era was simple and durable: classical statistical
tools, applied with engineering discipline to the right problem,
produce outsized results. Complexity for its own sake is a liability.
Rigor applied to the right question is a superpower.

## Financial Services

The systems perspective transferred naturally to financial markets.
To manage his own portfolio intelligently, Peck invested across every
major asset class — equities, fixed income, futures, mortgages, and
digital assets. Not as speculation. As education. The only way to
genuinely understand a financial system is to have real exposure to
it, watch it behave unexpectedly, and be forced to understand why.

The strategies that worked well became systematic. The systematic
approaches became funds. Peck launched a quantitative cryptocurrency
hedge fund, applying the same ML discipline he'd developed in
earlier work to digital asset markets. The trading worked. Attracting
conventional institutional capital required relationship skills that
were a poor fit — the glad-handing, the cocktail circuits, the
cold outreach that traditional fund-raising demands. After four years
and real experience on both sides of that problem, the fund closed.

The book emerged from the same period. Managing significant crypto
exposure required a risk framework that didn't exist. Classical
models borrowed from equity and fixed income weren't built for an
asset class with five distinct risk dimensions that traditional
finance had never encountered. Peck built the framework for his own
portfolio, then published it.

*Cryptocurrency Risk Management* was released in December 2022 by
Rethink Press. The same month, FTX collapsed — the largest
counterparty failure in crypto history. The book had mapped exactly
that risk category months before it materialized. It reached Amazon
bestseller status in the weeks that followed. CBS, Reuters, and the
Wall Street Journal needed a credible voice with a framework that
predated the crisis. The timing wasn't luck. It was what happens when
you build the framework because you actually need it.

## Researcher

The financial chapter is complete in the sense that matters. The
family is cared for. The portfolio is stable. The income is
sufficient. What remains is the research — and the recognition that
the research is worth doing for reasons that have nothing to do with
personal financial gain.

The tools that Peck benefitted from across his career — the
statistical frameworks, the systems architectures, the mathematical
methods for detecting structure in noisy data — were built by
researchers who came before. They didn't build them for Peck
specifically. They built them because the work needed doing, and
others would build on it. The goal now is to return the favor.

Financial markets are complex systems. Complex systems have structure.
That structure reveals itself in the data — in the way volatility
clusters, credit spreads widen, price momentum shifts, and macro
indicators align — if you have the right framework to see it.
Most participants don't. They are navigating by intuition and
narrative in a system that has already measured what they're guessing
at. The data is there. The understanding is not.

MacroContext is the current expression of that research program —
a live regime detection platform for financial markets, applying
classical machine learning to characterize which behavioral state
markets are currently in. Not predict. Characterize. The distinction
matters. The MacroContext Index Family extends that research into
investable products for institutional investors.

The AppliedComplexity Newsletter publishes the research as it
develops — the weekly classification, the signals behind it, and
the notes on methodology as it evolves. It is the public record of
the work in progress.

The open problem is the one that keeps the research moving: the
model characterizes states. It doesn't yet understand how sequences
of states tell a coherent story about the market environment. That
gap — between classification and narrative — is where the science
still needs to go.
