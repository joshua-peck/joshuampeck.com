01 · The Problem

Investors consistently misread which market environment they are operating in.

Financial media optimizes for engagement, not accuracy. The result is constant noise — every week is either a buying opportunity or a looming catastrophe. Most of it is neither.

The framing matters more than the forecast. An investor who believes they are in a benign market takes on more risk than the environment warrants. An investor convinced a crash is imminent sits in cash through a bull market.

The consequential question is not which stock to buy. It is which market environment you are currently in. Get that right and most allocation decisions follow.

Systematic regime detection replaces narrative with measurement. The model asks one question: what is the S&P 500 doing right now, and has it changed?

{{/* Critical framing callout */}}
Critical framing
  • This model does not detect economic recessions. It does not measure GDP, employment, or the business cycle.
  • It detects regime changes in the S&P 500 index — behavioral shifts in price action, volatility, and the macro indicators that have historically led those shifts.
  • An economic recession may or may not accompany a Danger classification. The model does not care. It cares about whether equity market conditions have deteriorated enough to warrant defensive positioning.
  • The distinction matters: the 2022 bear market occurred with no official recession. The 2001 recession produced a bear market. The model is calibrated to the equity market, not the economy.

03 · Why HMMs

HMMs match the statistical structure of how markets actually behave.

Markets don't jump between environments randomly. A bear market lasts months. A bull market lasts years. The HMM transition matrix encodes this directly: self-transition probabilities of 90–97% mean the model expects regimes to persist unless the data says otherwise.

The approach is pragmatic. HMMs were the first technique applied to this problem. They produced results worth publishing. The framework continues to develop.

Three states, not two: Boom, Neutral, Danger. Two-state models conflate quiet consolidation with strong bull markets — statistically different environments that warrant different treatment.

The regime is not directly observable. The model infers it from observable indicators. This is the correct framing for equity markets — there is no official announcement that a bear market has begun. The model attempts to detect it as it develops.

04 · The Approach

16 macro and market indicators, compressed to 4 factors, classified weekly since 2006.

{{/* ─── 4a — Feature selection ─── */}}
4a

Feature selection

Leading indicators over lagging ones — the model targets early detection, not confirmation.

This model is designed to detect equity market regime changes — not economic conditions. Feature selection reflects this. Features that lead equity price action are preferred over features that confirm it.

Lagging economic indicators (employment levels, GDP, industrial production) are intentionally excluded. By the time employment deteriorates, equity markets have typically already priced the recession.

Rate of change over level: a 5% fed funds rate stable for six months is not a stress signal. A rate that moved from 0% to 5% in twelve months is. The model captures this distinction.

{{/* ─── 4b — PCA ─── */}}
4b

Dimensionality reduction via PCA

16 correlated features compressed to 4 independent factors — 88% of variance explained.

The 16 features are correlated. VIX and credit spreads move together during stress. SPX and momentum indicators move together during bull markets. Feeding correlated features directly into the HMM inflates the parameter count and destabilizes training.

PCA compresses 16 correlated features into 4–5 uncorrelated principal components that capture 88% of total variance.

Two concrete benefits: reduces HMM parameter count from ~375 to ~60 (critical given 20 years of daily data), and removes noise while preserving the dominant macro signals.

PCA is fitted on training data only at each refit. No future data enters the compression step.

{{/* ─── 4c — HMM fitting ─── */}}
4c

HMM fitting

Three regimes, 20 random restarts, ordered by risk.

  • 3-state Gaussian HMM fitted on PCA components using Baum-Welch expectation-maximization.
  • 20 random restarts per fold — best-fitting solution selected by log-likelihood. Reduces sensitivity to local optima.
  • Diagonal covariance — a deliberate simplification that reduces parameters while preserving per-regime feature distributions.
  • After fitting, states are ordered by composite risk score derived from emission means. The state with elevated VIX, wide credit spreads, and SPX below trend becomes Danger. Strong momentum, low volatility becomes Boom. The middle state is Neutral.

{{/* ─── 4d — Walk-forward validation ─── */}}
4d

Walk-forward validation

Every historical classification was generated on data the model had never seen.

7 folds, 2019–2026. Each fold trains on all history through year Y, predicts year Y+1.

The 2020 COVID classifications were made by a model trained through December 2019. The 2022 bear market by a model trained through December 2021.

This is not a backtest. A backtest trains on the full dataset and scores the same dataset — optimistic by construction. Walk-forward validation generates each classification on genuinely unseen data.

05 · Validation Results

Validated against conventional bear markets, with results published honestly.

{{/* ─── 5a — Ground truth ─── */}}
5a

What we measured against

Validated against conventional bear markets, not every 10% pullback.

Ground truth is defined as conventional bear markets: −20% peak-to-trough or sustained drawdown over two months. This matches the standard used by practitioners, not the narrower −10% threshold used in prior validation runs.

The prior threshold flagged short, sharp events (European Debt Crisis, Q4 2018, China/Oil Selloff) that resolved quickly without meeting conventional bear market criteria. The model largely missed these — correctly, under the revised standard.

This is an equity market model. The ground truth events are equity market events. Economic recessions that did not produce sustained equity drawdowns are not validation targets.

{{/* ─── 5d — Known failures ─── */}}
5d

Known failures

The model catches sustained bear markets and misses short, sharp drawdowns.

The formal validation fails four of four pass/fail checks at the current thresholds. The equity curve and major drawdown detection are strong. The coverage and stability metrics identify where the model still has work to do.

Short-duration events (COVID crash: 23 trading days) push the limits of weekly classification. The model is designed for sustained regime changes, not sudden shocks.

Regime stability varies across folds. The 17.5% agreement between Folds 2 and 3 reflects COVID entering the training window for the first time — reshuffling historical labels significantly. Stability is high (90%+) once both GFC and COVID are in the training window.

6b

Mahalanobis distance to each regime

Each regime has a statistical signature — what the 4 PCA factors look like when the model is in that state.

The current day's reading is compared to each regime's signature. The Mahalanobis distance measures how many standard deviations the current reading is from each regime center.

The regime with the smallest distance is the best fit. This is the model's reasoning made visible.

07 · Known Limitations

Four limitations the research is actively working on.

Limitation 01

Regime stability on refit

Historical labels can shift when major new events enter the training window. Active area of development. Bayesian formulation expected to improve this.

Limitation 02

Short-event detection

Calibrated for sustained drawdowns. Fast, sharp events (23 trading days) push the limits of weekly classification. Options market signals not currently in the feature set may help.

Limitation 03

Feature selection look-ahead

The choice of which 16 indicators to include was made with knowledge of the full history. True prospective validation began April 2026.

Limitation 04

Three states may not be sufficient

The model does not yet distinguish inflationary stress (2022 rate hike bear) from deflationary stress (2008 GFC) — different environments that may warrant different responses.

Three enhancements in active development.

Next 01

Bayesian HMM via PyMC

Continuous probability output (0–1 risk index) rather than hard regime labels. The foundation for the MacroContext Transition Probability Index.

Next 02

Revised ground truth

Aligning validation to conventional bear market definitions. Coverage scores improve substantially under the revised standard. Full re-validation in progress.
