Executive Summary

A systematic drawdown detection engine, not a return predictor.

MacroContext is a quantitative framework that identifies prevailing market regimes using a combination of macro and market stress indicators. By systematically reducing equity exposure during confirmed stress periods and maintaining full exposure during normal and recovery conditions, the framework has historically produced materially better risk-adjusted returns than a buy-and-hold approach — with nearly double the Sharpe ratio, 38% less volatility, and a maximum drawdown 40% shallower than unhedged equity exposure.

The core insight is not that we can predict market direction. It is that we can systematically characterize the current macro and market environment, and size positions accordingly. The model does one job well: detecting when conditions have deteriorated enough to warrant defensive positioning.

The Problem

Buy-and-hold is the right default. The exceptions are what matter.

Equity markets spend the vast majority of time trending higher. Buy-and-hold is the correct default posture. But periodically — roughly 10–15% of all trading days historically — conditions deteriorate sharply enough that unhedged equity exposure becomes deeply unfavorable. The 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the 2020 COVID crash, and the 2022 rate hike bear market each produced drawdowns exceeding 25% from peak to trough.

The challenge is not identifying these events in hindsight. Everyone knows the GFC was a crisis. The challenge is detecting deteriorating conditions early enough to reduce exposure before the worst damage occurs — without generating so many false positives that defensive positioning erodes long-run returns.

Most approaches fail in one of two ways: react too slowly, triggering after most of the damage has occurred, or react too aggressively, producing excessive trading costs and missed upside from false alarms.

MacroContext was designed to find the middle path: a slow-moving, macro-anchored signal that is wrong rarely, and early when it is.

Design decision · rate of change

Rate features (DGS10, DTB3) are expressed as 3-month changes rather than absolute levels. The absolute level of interest rates does not predict equity stress — the rate of change does.

A 5% fed funds rate that has been stable for six months is not a stress signal. A rate that moved from 0% to 5% in twelve months is. This distinction resolved a persistent misclassification of 2024 — a strong bull market year where rates were elevated but stable — as a stress period.

Lagging economic indicators (employment levels, GDP, industrial production) are intentionally excluded. By the time employment deteriorates, equity markets have typically already priced the recession. The model targets early detection, not confirmation.

The Pipeline

Four steps from raw FRED series to regime classification.

{{/* STEP 01 */}}
01

PCA dimensionality reduction

The 16 features are correlated — VIX and credit spreads tend to move together during stress; SPX and momentum indicators tend to move together during bull markets. Principal Component Analysis compresses these 16 correlated features into 4–5 uncorrelated principal components that capture approximately 85% of the total variance.

This step is critical. It reduces the parameter count from roughly 375 to approximately 60 for the subsequent HMM fitting — dramatically improving statistical stability. PCA is fitted on training data only at each walk-forward refit; no future information enters the compression step.

{{/* STEP 02 */}}
02

Gaussian HMM with diagonal covariance

A 3-state Gaussian Hidden Markov Model is fitted on the PCA-compressed features using the Baum-Welch expectation-maximization algorithm. The HMM models each regime as a multivariate Gaussian distribution over the principal components, with a transition matrix that encodes the probability of moving between regimes on any given day.

  • Self-transition probabilities of ~90–97% — regimes persist for weeks to months, consistent with how macro conditions actually evolve.
  • Diagonal covariance — a deliberate simplification that reduces parameters while maintaining the ability to capture regime-specific feature distributions.
  • 20 random restarts — the best-fitting solution across 20 random initializations is selected, reducing sensitivity to local optima in the EM algorithm.
{{/* STEP 03 */}}
03

Regime ordering by composite risk score

HMM states are assigned arbitrary numeric labels during fitting. After each refit, states are ordered by a composite risk score derived from the emission means — the average feature values within each state. States where VIX is elevated, credit spreads are wide, and SPX is below trend are labeled Danger. States where momentum is strong and volatility is low are labeled Boom. The middle state becomes Mid-Market.

{{/* STEP 04 */}}
04

Walk-forward validation

The model is validated using an expanding-window walk-forward framework across seven folds covering 2019–2026. Each fold trains on all data up to a cutoff date and predicts on the following year — the model never sees future data during training. Feature matrices are recomputed at each cutoff date to eliminate look-ahead bias in the normalization step.

Position Sizing

Mid-Market gets 100%. Boom gets 80%. Counterintuitive — and intentional.

Mid-Market represents the normal, steady majority of market history — periods where regular uncertainty is the backdrop and markets trend higher without exceptional conditions in either direction. Full exposure during this state captures the fundamental equity risk premium efficiently.

The Boom regime — despite having the highest trailing Sharpe ratio — carries elevated transition risk. The periods the model classifies as Boom include both clear melt-ups and late-cycle peak conditions, both characterized by the same feature signature: strong momentum, low volatility, price near recent highs. These conditions immediately precede many significant drawdowns. The GFC began from what appeared to be favorable conditions. COVID hit markets near all-time highs.

The 80% sizing during Boom reflects this asymmetry: capture most of the upside during momentum periods, but maintain a small defensive buffer against the elevated probability of regime transition.

Sensitivity analysis

Sizing Boom anywhere from 75% to 100% produces nearly identical Sharpe ratios (0.90–0.92), with the primary variation in maximum drawdown. The model earns its value at the extremes, not in the middle.

Where The Model Fails

Four limitations under active calibration.

These are documented honestly because the user of the model needs to know its blind spots. Each is being actively addressed in the current development cycle.

Limitation 01

Danger overrepresentation

The current model assigns ~30% of trading days to Danger, vs an empirical historical rate of roughly 10–12% for confirmed 10%+ drawdown periods. This reflects a tendency to call elevated-but-resolving stress as Danger rather than transitioning cleanly back to Mid-Market. Ongoing work on transition matrix priors and base rate constraints is expected to improve this.

Limitation 02

Fast geopolitical shocks

The model struggled to classify the 2025 tariff stress and 2026 Iran war as Danger, assigning them to Mid-Market instead. Both events featured rapid onset and relatively fast resolution compared to the GFC and 2022 bear market. Severe-but-brief events may require additional detection mechanisms — potentially incorporating options market signals not currently in the feature set.

Limitation 03

Regime stability across refits

When a major new macro event enters the training window for the first time — particularly COVID entering fold 3 — historical labels can shift significantly. This reflects the HMM's sensitivity to the full distribution of training data rather than a flaw in specific fold predictions. Stability is high (90%+) across folds 3–7 once the model has seen both GFC and COVID.

Limitation 04

Look-ahead in feature engineering

Equity curve results use walk-forward OOS predictions, but the feature engineering choices — which features to include, which transformations to apply — were made with knowledge of the full history. Standard practice in systematic strategy development, but a form of look-ahead that cannot be fully eliminated without a true out-of-sample holdout. Live trading from April 2026 forward is the first genuinely prospective validation.

Theoretical Foundation

Built on Hamilton (1989), extended in three directions.

The use of Hidden Markov Models for financial regime detection follows a well-established literature originating with Hamilton (1989). The core assumption — that markets transition between distinct behavioral states governed by unobservable latent variables — is supported by decades of empirical evidence showing that asset return distributions are non-stationary and regime-dependent.

The MacroContext framework extends this foundation in three specific ways:

Feature selection toward leading indicators

Where the academic literature frequently uses returns and volatility as HMM inputs — which are by definition concurrent with the regime — MacroContext uses macro and market stress indicators with empirical leading relationships to drawdown events. Credit spreads, yield curve shape, and volatility trends provide earlier signals than price-derived features alone.

PCA preprocessing for stability

Applying dimensionality reduction before HMM fitting reduces parameter count, removes correlated noise, and dramatically improves walk-forward stability. This preprocessing step is underutilized in the applied literature and represents a significant practical contribution.

Price-derived ground truth

Validating against objectively defined peak-to-trough drawdown events rather than self-referential model outputs provides a clean separation between model design and model validation — a discipline absent from much of the regime detection literature.

Roadmap

The current model is a validated proof of concept.

Four planned enhancements move the framework from a hard-classification HMM toward a continuous, explainable, and deeper-history risk index.

Next 01

Bayesian inference via PyMC

Migration from frequentist Baum-Welch EM to a full Bayesian HMM will enable posterior probability outputs — a continuous risk index between 0 and 1 — rather than hard regime assignments. This is the foundation for the MacroContext Transition Probability Index (TPI), the early warning signal that will anchor the weekly publication.

Next 02

SHAP attribution

Integrating SHAP explainability will enable per-day attribution of the regime signal to individual features. The weekly publication will show not just the current regime but the top contributing factors — "today's Danger signal is driven 40% by credit spread widening and 35% by VIX trend."

Next 03

Extended history

Replacing late-starting FRED series with longer-history proxies will extend the training window back to approximately 1991 — adding the 1990 recession, 1994 rate shock, 1998 LTCM crisis, and 2001 dot-com bust as additional regime transitions. More distinct crisis examples improve generalization.

Next 04

Live validation

The model runs in production from April 2026, with weekly regime calls published publicly under the MacroContext and AppliedComplexity brands. The prospective track record, beginning from the current Iran war stress period, will constitute the first fully out-of-sample validation.
